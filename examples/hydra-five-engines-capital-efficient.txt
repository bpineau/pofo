# Hydra (five engines, capital-efficient) -- the frontier build.
#
# STATUS: research portfolio, NOT a buy list. Two engines live in US-listed
#   funds with no PRIIPs KID (RSBT, GDE; KMLM and ZROZ too), so an EU retail
#   investor cannot implement this today. It exists (a) to mark where the
#   efficient frontier of this engine philosophy actually sits, (b) as the
#   canonical rationale file for the hydra family, and (c) as a WATCH LIST:
#   if a UCITS twin of the missing pieces ever launches, this is the build
#   it unlocks. The implementable version is hydra-five-engines.txt, which
#   gives up ~30-40 points of notional to stay buyable.
#
# Thesis in one line: the same five engines as the buyable hydra, but every
#   sleeve that CAN be expressed through an embedded-leverage instrument is,
#   so ~148% of notional exposure fits in 100% of capital with no margin
#   account, no borrow rate spread, and no daily-reset decay.
#
# ## What 100 EUR actually holds (the exposure ledger)
#
#   35 NTSG   -> 31.5 world equity + 21 sovereign futures (~7y duration)
#   12 ZPRV   -> 12   US small-cap value equity
#   16 RSBT   -> 16   US core bonds + 16 managed-futures trend
#   14 KMLM   -> 14   trend (MLM index, no equity futures)
#   18 GDE    -> 16.2 US large-cap equity + 16.2 gold futures
#    5 ZROZ   ->  5   US Treasuries at ~27y duration
#   ------------------------------------------------------------------
#   Total     ~60 equity + ~42 bonds + ~30 trend + ~16 gold  = ~148%
#
#   The extra ~48 points are financed inside the funds at roughly the
#   T-bill rate (futures carry), the cheapest leverage a retail investor
#   can reach and the whole point of the build: the diversifiers stack ON
#   TOP of a full growth engine instead of being funded by shrinking it.
#
# ## Why embedded leverage, not external leverage
#   The same ~148% book could be assembled from plain funds plus external
#   financing: a margin/Lombard loan on the CTO, short box spreads, or a
#   DIY futures overlay (hydra-five-engines-levered.txt backtests that
#   route at the cash rate + 1%). Embedded stacking wins on five fronts:
#   1. NO MARGIN CALL, EVER. External leverage is path-dependent: a deep
#      drawdown raises the account's leverage ratio exactly when prices
#      are lowest, and the broker (whose risk desk can also RAISE margin
#      requirements overnight mid-crisis) forces sales at the bottom. A
#      90/60 fund rebalances its own overlay internally; its NAV can
#      drop, but the holder can never be liquidated out of the position.
#      For a decumulation portfolio this is the decisive property:
#      forced deleveraging at the low is sequence risk squared.
#   2. LEVERAGE RATIO BEHAVIOR. A loan is a FIXED liability against a
#      falling asset: leverage drifts UP in drawdowns (anti-convex, the
#      dangerous direction) unless the investor actively deleverages at
#      the worst moment. The funds keep notional ratios roughly constant
#      by design, without the daily-reset volatility decay of 2x/3x ETFs.
#   3. FINANCING RATE. Futures carry prices at ~T-bill, the wholesale
#      rate. Retail margin runs benchmark +1 to +3%; only disciplined
#      box-spread selling gets near ~+0.2-0.5%, and it must be re-rolled
#      at maturity at whatever the new rate is, with options spreads and
#      portfolio-margin requirements on top.
#   4. TAX (French CTO). Margin interest is NOT deductible against PFU-
#      taxed gains: a pure after-tax dead loss. A box spread's implicit
#      interest at least realizes as an offsettable capital loss. Inside
#      a fund, financing nets against returns BEFORE NAV, i.e. fully
#      pre-tax, automatically. Embedded > box > margin, tax-wise.
#   5. BALANCE SHEET AND OPERATIONS. Fund leverage is invisible to the
#      investor's personal balance sheet: no debt line eating mortgage
#      capacity (taux d'endettement), no rolls to execute, no expiries
#      to diarize, nothing to mis-execute in a volatile week. The fund
#      does daily, at institutional cost, what a retail investor does
#      badly quarterly.
#   Honest counterpoints: a well-run box-spread program costs about what
#   the stacked funds' TER premium does (RSBT is the pricey one), so the
#   case rests on points 1, 2, 4 and 5, not on raw cost; options and
#   daily-reset leveraged ETFs DO charge an embedded-leverage premium
#   (Frazzini & Pedersen, "Embedded Leverage", 2022), which is why only
#   futures-overlay funds qualify for this build; and external leverage
#   can lever ANY mix, including UCITS-only sleeves, while stacking only
#   exists in the combinations issuers ship -- which is exactly why this
#   file doubles as a watch list, and why the levered sibling exists.
#
# ## Why engines, and why these mechanics
#
# 1. DIVERSIFY ACROSS RETURN SOURCES. Combining lowly-correlated streams
#    that each carry positive expected return is what lifts risk-adjusted
#    return, far more than adding names does (Ilmanen & Kizer, "The Death
#    of Diversification Has Been Greatly Exaggerated", JPM 2012;
#    Choueifaty & Coignard, "Toward Maximum Diversification", JPM 2008).
#    Each head is a distinct, documented return source driven by a
#    different macro force, so their worst years rarely coincide.
# 2. SIZE BY RISK CONTRIBUTION, NOT CAPITAL (Qian, "Risk Parity
#    Portfolios", PanAgora 2005; Bridgewater's All Weather): balance
#    exposures across growth/inflation/deflation outcomes, not dollars
#    across tickers. The ledger above is the point: capital weights and
#    exposures are different objects.
# 3. CAPITAL EFFICIENCY. A modestly levered balanced mix beats a
#    concentrated equity portfolio at equal risk (Asness, "Why Not 100%
#    Equities", JPM 1996; Frazzini & Pedersen, "Betting Against Beta",
#    JFE 2014: leverage aversion leaves the diversified mix underpriced).
#    Return stacking (Hoffstein, Butler & Gordillo; WisdomTree Efficient
#    Core) is the packaged form: NTSG 90/60, RSBT 100/100, GDE 90/90,
#    and a zero-coupon's duration-per-euro on the defensive sleeve.
#
# ## Regime map: what hurts, and which head answers
#
#   Growth boom            ~60% equity across three engines (NTSG world,
#                          ZPRV small value, GDE US large): full presence.
#   Disinflation/recession ~37% intermediate bonds (NTSG overlay + RSBT
#                          core) + trend rotating long duration.
#   Deflation crash (2008) ZROZ long-end convexity + trend short risk
#                          assets + unhedged USD flight-to-quality.
#   Inflation shock        Trend long commodities/short bonds (Neville,
#                          Draaisma, Funnell, Harvey & van Hemert, "The
#                          Best Strategies for Inflationary Times", JPM
#                          2021) + 16% gold via GDE.
#   Choppy stagflation     Gold (trend whipsaws without a trend; Neville
#                          et al. 2021).
#   Prolonged equity bear  Trend crisis alpha (Hurst, Ooi & Pedersen, "A
#                          Century of Evidence on Trend-Following
#                          Investing", JPM 2017).
#
#   Measured coverage (share of weight): growth 60%, inflation 58%,
#   crisis 51%, deflation 42% -- every quadrant fatter than the buyable
#   hydra (44/44/37/26), which is what the extra notional buys.
#
# ## The five engines: rationale, sane ranges, UCITS status
#
# ENGINE 1 -- NTSG, equity + duration (35%; sane range 30-45%).
#   90% developed-world equities + 60% sovereign futures in one line.
#   Already UCITS (IE00077IIPQ8); the euro-native NTSZ (IE000OV4XWA3) is
#   the FX-aware variant. This is the one engine that survives unchanged
#   in the buyable sibling.
#
# ENGINE 2 -- ZPRV, small-cap value (12%; sane range 8-15%).
#   Size and value premia (Fama & French 1992/1993) in the value-weighted
#   form that avoids the small-growth lottery corner (Asness, Frazzini,
#   Israel, Moskowitz & Pedersen, "Size Matters, If You Control Your
#   Junk", JFE 2018). Deepest clean backcast of the book (DFSVX spliced
#   onto Ken French small-value, 1963). Already UCITS (IE00BSPLC413);
#   global variant AVWS (IE0003R87OG3).
#
# ENGINES 3a/3b -- trend, TWO models (16% + 14%; range 20-35% total, no
#   single leg above ~20%).
#   Time-series momentum (Moskowitz, Ooi & Pedersen, JFE 2012) is the one
#   head that can profit in both tails, and the sleeve with the worst
#   single-model risk, so it is split across two genuinely different
#   methods. Engine 3a rides INSIDE RSBT (SG CTA replication a la DBi)
#   stacked on core bonds, so the trend budget costs no capital; engine
#   3b is the rules-based MLM index (KMLM), which holds no equity futures
#   and stays clean in equity crashes. NEITHER is UCITS. The unstacked
#   UCITS routes: DBMFE (LU2951555403) for 3a, AQR Managed Futures UCITS
#   (LU1103257975, the standout live record 2015-2026) or Winton
#   (IE00BG382Q20) for 3b -- see the wider-universe note below.
#
# ENGINE 4 -- GDE, gold stacked on equity (18%; sane range 10-22%).
#   Gold alone has near-zero expected real return (Erb & Harvey, "The
#   Golden Dilemma", FAJ 2013) -- the classic argument for keeping it
#   small. GDE dodges the drag by stacking 90% gold futures on 90% S&P
#   500, so the sleeve keeps earning the equity premium while carrying
#   the debasement/stagflation hedge. That is why its sane range runs
#   wider than plain gold (the sweep shows Sharpe flat to ~22% and ruin
#   still falling); the price is ~16 more points of US large-cap beta.
#   NOT UCITS, and no UCITS twin exists: the fallback is a physical ETC
#   (IGLN IE00B4ND3602) at ~0.9x, accepting the drag GDE was dodging.
#
# ENGINE 5 -- ZROZ, deep deflation duration (5%; sane range 3-8%).
#   ~27y duration per euro vs ~17 for a 20+y fund: capital efficiency
#   applied to crash insurance. NTSG and RSBT carry intermediate
#   duration; this is the long end, which is what actually rallies in a
#   2008. NOT UCITS; route: IDTL (IE00BSKRJZ44) at ~1.6x, or EUR-hedged
#   DTLE to strip the USD from the defensive sleeve.
#
# ## Stacked alternatives for the three PLAIN sleeves, checked and rejected
#   KMLM, ZPRV and ZROZ are the sleeves NOT already return-stacked. The
#   test: swap each for a stacked instrument carrying the same core plus a
#   free second exposure, same weights, 1988-2026 EUR:
#   - Trend (KMLM) -> RSST (US stocks + trend) or Winton Trend Enhanced
#     Global Equity (MSCI World + trend). Both raise CAGR, but Sharpe
#     falls (0.87 -> 0.84 / 0.82), vol and drawdown rise, worst 5y CAGR
#     collapses (1.6% -> 0.4% / -0.1%) and recovery stretches to 3.7-4.7y.
#     The book already carries ~60 points of equity; the trend sleeve
#     earns its keep precisely by NOT adding more. KMLM stays plain.
#   - Small-cap value (ZPRV): no stacked SCV instrument exists. The
#     nearest same-core alternative is global SCV (Avantis, DPGT); on the
#     common 1994 window it is a wash (Sharpe 0.89 vs 0.88). ZPRV keeps
#     the deeper clean backcast (1963 vs 1994), so it stays.
#   - Long Treasury (ZROZ): a 25+y zero-coupon is ALREADY the most
#     capital-efficient long-duration expression. Nothing to swap to.
#   Net: the plain sleeves are plain on purpose. Stacking is used where
#   it helps (growth, duration, gold cores), not on the crisis hedges.
#
# ## Wider-universe search on the same three sleeves (same weights)
#   - Long duration vs EURO govt: on 2008-2026 the USD 25+y zero-coupon
#     beats the iShares euro 15-30y govt (IE00B1FZS913) on CAGR, Sharpe,
#     worst-5y and drawdown; euro govt has ~16y duration vs 27 and no USD
#     flight-to-quality kick. IDTL lands between the two. ZROZ stays.
#   - ZPRV vs broad small cap: on 2018-2026 dropping the value tilt LOSES
#     (Sharpe 0.85 vs 0.88, worst 5y 7.0% vs 7.9%). The size+value
#     combination does the work; broad small cap is not an upgrade.
#     Deepening global SCV below ~1994 would proxy non-US with US data
#     and fake the diversification that was the point. ZPRV stays.
#   - Trend model choice barely matters in the backcast (all deep trend
#     histories share one TSMOM proxy: KMLM->AQR is a wash there), but
#     the LIVE record separates them sharply: over 2022-2026 KMLM was the
#     worst of the group (Sharpe ~0, drawdown -42%) while AQR Managed
#     Futures UCITS ran Sharpe 0.75 with a -20% max drawdown. For any
#     real holding, prefer AQR; KMLMSIM stays as the backtest proxy only.
#
# ## Blind spots (the buyable hydra's, plus the leverage-specific ones)
#
#   (a) DEEPER CRISIS DRAWDOWNS, measured: vs the buyable hydra, real
#       drawdowns of -24% vs -21% (2000-02), -20% vs -13% (2008), -16.5%
#       vs -13.3% (2022). ~148% notional and ~60% equity mean the frontier
#       build is the BULL-market frontier, not the defensive one; beta vs
#       S&P is 0.59 vs 0.47 and down-capture 62% vs 50%. The compensation
#       is faster recoveries (2.8y worst) and a higher floor (worst 5y
#       +1.6%), not shallower dips.
#   (b) Embedded financing cost: the ~48 stacked points pay T-bill inside
#       the funds. When cash is at 5% that is ~2.4%/yr of drag before the
#       overlay earns anything, exactly when bonds and equities may both
#       struggle (2022-23). Invisible on a statement, real in the return.
#   (c) Fee stack: 0.46%/yr weighted TER vs 0.26% for the buyable hydra.
#   (d) Product risk: RSBT and GDE are small, young ETFs; a closure or a
#       mandate change forces an unplanned redesign (and a taxable event).
#   (e) A fast joint stock/bond selloff that whipsaws trend before it
#       flips short: with 42 points of bonds and 60 of equity, this build
#       leans harder on trend reacting in time than the buyable one.
#   (f) Currency: every line is USD-quoted and unhedged for a EUR
#       investor; euro-area inflation only hedged indirectly; no euro
#       liquid sleeve for decumulation. Same patches as the buyable
#       hydra (NTSZ, DTLE, euro linkers near departure).
#   (g) Reconstruction risk: most of the deep history is backcast (SIM),
#       and the trend backcasts understate live manager dispersion. Treat
#       the backtest as a regime-behavior study, not a promise.
#
# ## Honesty note on the WEIGHTS
#   The sleeves and their roles are theory-first; the exact percentages
#   are backtest-informed (optimizer agreement over 1988-2026, hand-
#   tempered, tuned against claude-dragonlite). Expect the measured edge
#   to shrink out of sample; the engine logic and the ranges are the part
#   built to survive. RANGE CHECK (per-sleeve sweep on THIS build, rest
#   renormalized, 1988-2026 + bootstrap ruin at 4% real WR over 40y):
#   confirmed empirically -- ZPRV 8-15 (worst-5y peaks at 12, max
#   drawdown -37% by 25), the trend FLOOR at 20% (at 0% trend: Sharpe
#   0.76, worst-5y -2.2%, drawdown -39%, 5y recovery, ruin x3.5), the
#   NTSG CEILING near 45% (worst-5y negative by 55%, recovery 2.8 ->
#   4.8y), GDE 10-22 (below 10 ruin doubles; worst-5y fades past 22),
#   ZROZ floor at 3 (0% costs worst-5y and drawdown). Still A PRIORI:
#   the trend ceiling (the backtest is happy at 45%: in-sample flattery
#   of the shared TSMOM backcast) and the NTSG floor (the window does
#   not punish 15% NTSG because GDE and the backcast-flattered
#   diversifiers carried it; forward-looking, the world-equity engine
#   pays the plan).
#
# ## Measured profile (this toolkit, EUR, common window 1988-11 -> 2026-07)
#   CAGR 12.08%, vol 14.0%, Sharpe 0.87, Sortino 1.25, max drawdown
#   -34.4% with a 2.8y worst recovery, worst rolling 5y +1.58%, median 5y
#   10.74%, up/down-capture 66/62%. Vs the buyable hydra: +1.5 pts of
#   CAGR, +0.55 pt of worst-5y, +12 pts of up-capture, at the cost of
#   +1.6 pts of vol, deeper crisis drawdowns (see (a)) and +0.20%/yr of
#   fees. That gap IS the current price of the UCITS constraint.
#   Decumulation check (monthly real returns, stationary bootstrap, 40y):
#   ruin at a 4% real WR 0.2%, safe WR at 5% ruin 6.3% vs 5.8% buyable.
#   Read the LEVELS skeptically (the panel compounds ~10.6%/yr real, far
#   above any sane forward anchor); read the GAP comparatively.
