Method & honest caveats · why these figures can look optimistic

Reference points: the classic US 4%/30y backtest ≈ 95% success; broad century-long developed-market samples give ≈ 75-85% for the same fixed 4% rule (Anarkulova, Cederburg & O'Doherty), with a ~2.3% safe rate at 5% ruin. These models are i.i.d. (no mean reversion), so long FIRE horizons read a little stricter than history.

These results are model-based and tend to be more optimistic than the empirical safe-withdrawal evidence. The main reasons, in rough order of impact:

  • Dynamic spending. The "spending cut in downturns" (flex) reduces withdrawals in bad markets, which roughly halves ruin vs a true fixed rule. It defaults to 0 (the canonical fixed rule); section 02 shows what enabling it costs in lived spending.
  • Optimistic return inputs. A μ/σ fitted from a fund's own history reflects a favourable, often post-2009 window. The Broad-sample and Lost-decade columns supply the deep-past counterpoint; the central case is automatically blended toward the broad-sample prior when the fund history is shorter than the horizon.
  • I.i.d. draws miss the persistence of real returns; the Sequence-stress column clusters bad years at the same long-run mean to expose that risk separately from the return level.
  • Taxes. The single-CTO default grosses up every sale at the flat rate on gains; the PEA/AV envelope sliders model the real French multi-envelope sequencing, which is materially cheaper.
  • Mortality. The ruin figures ignore death by default (a 45-year horizon is survived with certainty); section 03 shows the mortality-weighted picture, which is kinder, and more honest about late-life failures.

For a life plan, read the pessimistic columns, keep the horizon past your life expectancy, and treat the ruin figures as ordinal (compare scenarios, don't trust decimals).
