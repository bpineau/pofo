Read the ruin figures with care — why they can look optimistic

These results are model-based and tend to be more optimistic than the empirical safe-withdrawal evidence (e.g. Anarkulova, Cederburg & O'Doherty, 2023, who find a fixed 4% rule fails far more often across a broad, century-long sample of developed markets). The main reasons, in rough order of impact:

  • Dynamic spending. The "spending cut in downturns" (flex) reduces the withdrawal in downturns, which roughly halves the ruin vs a true fixed 4% rule. It now defaults to 0 (the canonical fixed rule); raise it, or use the guardrails toggle, to model adaptive spending.
  • Optimistic return inputs. mu/sigma fitted from a fund's own history reflect a favourable, often US-heavy and post-2009 window. A broad sample has a lower real return, higher volatility and a fatter left tail (prolonged real bear markets like Japan 1990–).
  • Short history. A 20–30-year series contains no independent 40–50-year retirement, so it cannot show the worst long-horizon sequences. A warning appears above when the sample is shorter than the horizon.
  • I.i.d. parametric draws miss the persistence (sequence risk) and negative skew of real returns; they under-produce the early-crash paths that actually cause ruin.
  • Horizon. Lengthen it for an early retirement (longevity): the ruin rises steeply with years.

For a life plan, prefer the pessimistic end: flex off, a lower mu and higher sigma than the rosy fit, and a horizon past your life expectancy.
